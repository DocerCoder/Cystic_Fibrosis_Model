  • The sweat chloride measurement is a test used to help diagnose CF.
  • In general, the higher a patient's sweat chloride is, the more likely the patient is to have CF.
  • Adults who do not have CF almost always have a sweat chloride measurement of less than 60 mEq/L.

  • The lung function of all patients with CF is impaired, but how severe the lung disease is can vary from patient to patient. The lung function of CF patients usually gets worse over time.
  • Pulmonary function tests, such as the Forced Expiratory Volume test (FEV1%), are one way we can measure a patient's lung function to see how the patient is doing and keep track of the patient's lung function.
  • This test measures the amount of air that a person blows out in the first second when he or she forcefully exhales (breathes out) after taking as deep a breath as possible.
  • We compare that number to the number that we would predict for someone of the same age, height, gender, and race with normal lung function. Some people may have an FEV1% that is greater than 100% of what is predicted for their age, height, gender, and race.
  • Using this comparison, doctors then report the percent (%) predicted of FEV1% for that patient.

Please note:

There is a wide range in lung function in all CF patients. There also is a wide range in lung function in patients with the same variant combination.

Because of this variability, it is important to use caution when interpreting the lung function results of this website. We encourage you to discuss this information with your doctor.

The clinical data collected by CFTR2 likely reflects different CF care practices that vary by country, region, or territory. There may be differences in availability of treatments, including CFTR modulators. Some individuals in this dataset may have been taking CFTR modulators at the time that their data was collected, while others were not. Use of CFTR modulators and other treatments may impact the clinical data shown here.

  • Many CF patients are pancreatic insufficient, which means they need to take pancreatic enzyme supplements to replace the enzymes that their bodies do not make.
  • These enzymes help their bodies to absorb the nutrients and vitamins that they eat.
  • The percentage (%) listed here is the percentage of patients with this variant combination who are pancreatic insufficient and need to take pancreatic replacement enzymes.

  • There are many types of infections that affect the lungs of patients with CF.
  • The Pseudomonas aeruginosa bacteria is the most common cause of lung infections in CF patients, particularly as their lung disease progresses.
  • Persistent infection with pseudomonas is associated with worsening lung disease.
  • CF patients who have had infections with the Pseudomonas aeruginosa bacteria may have to take specialized antibiotics.
  • The percentage (%) listed here is the percentage of patients with this variant in the CFTR2 database who have had at least 1 previous Pseudomonas infection and at the time the information was collected have Pseudomonas in their sputum (and therefore are infected with the bacteria).
